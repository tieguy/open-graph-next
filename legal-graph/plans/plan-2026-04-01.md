## Constitutional Caselaw Knowledge Graph — Prototype Sketch

### Conceptual Anchor: Semantic Scholar for Law

The Allen Institute’s [Semantic Scholar](https://www.semanticscholar.org) demonstrates that a large document corpus can be transformed into a structured knowledge layer by extracting citation context, classifying citation intent, and making the result queryable in ways the raw corpus doesn’t support. Their [S2ORC pipeline](https://github.com/allenai/s2orc) structures full-text papers; their citation intent classifier labels edges as background/method/result; [OpenScholar](https://github.com/AkariAsai/OpenScholar) uses the result to help researchers find gaps in literature coverage.

This prototype extends that model to constitutional caselaw, with three differences:

- **Domain**: Law rather than science — citation intent categories become affirming/distinguishing/overruling/background/procedure
- **Corpus**: Fully public domain (US federal caselaw via [Free Law Project](https://free.law)), not limited to open-access papers
- **Verification**: SIFT-based process producing a verified annotation layer — with human approval as a baseline to test against, not assumed to be necessary at scale
- **Output license**: CC0, enabling reuse as AI training data

-----

### The Three Outputs

**1. A verified benchmark dataset for legal citation accuracy** — training and evaluation data for the task “does this AI-generated legal claim accurately characterize its cited source,” built on public domain opinions, released under CC0. Addresses the worst failure mode of commercial legal AI (Harvey, Lexis+AI, Westlaw AI), which current benchmarks don’t adequately test.

**2. A demonstration that the method generalizes to normative/interpretive knowledge** — caselaw is harder than drug interactions (ground truth is contested) but more tractable than fully open-ended normative questions. Proving the method here extends its credibility to the harder domains the broader project needs.

**3. A structured knowledge base enabling LLM-assisted gap-finding for Wikipedia legal articles** — not content generation, but a queryable layer that helps editors identify where Wikipedia’s legal coverage is thin, unsourced, or inaccurate, and surfaces primary sources to fill those gaps. The legal analog to what OpenScholar does for scientific literature.

-----

### What Free Law Project Provides vs. What This Adds

[Free Law Project](https://free.law) has built the citation graph — opinion A cites opinion B, with character offsets. That’s the edge. What nobody has in open infrastructure is the *semantic content* of the edge: what proposition does A take from B, and is that characterization accurate? This project adds the verified annotation layer on top of FLP’s corpus — the same relationship as OpenCitations to Crossref. FLP is the upstream data provider; this is the semantic layer they’ve deliberately left out of scope.

-----

### The Stack

**Document layer — Free Law Project**

- Full text of US federal caselaw, public domain; bulk processed corpus available directly from FLP
- [Eyecite](https://github.com/freelawproject/eyecite) (FLP’s own citation parser) used to enumerate all citations within the seed case corpus reliably — not to do semantic analysis, just to ensure complete coverage of the citation graph within the test suite
- Parallel: [Harvard Caselaw Access Project](https://case.law) for historical depth

**Entity layer — Wikidata**

- Cases, courts, justices already partially represented; Q-IDs as persistent identifiers
- Gaps to fill: holding statements, doctrinal relationships, citation intent edges (P2860)

**Verification layer — LLM extraction + human baseline**

- Core research question: given opinion text containing a citation, can an LLM reliably identify the proposition for which the cited case is used, classify the relationship type (affirming/distinguishing/overruling/background/procedure), and locate the supporting passage in the cited opinion?
- Human verification used to build a ground-truth baseline for the seed cases — not assumed necessary at scale, but required to measure LLM accuracy
- Interesting failure modes are legal-specific: hallucinating that a case stands for a proposition it doesn’t hold; mischaracterizing a distinguishing as an affirming; correctly identifying the citation intent but locating the wrong passage in the cited opinion
- Key open question: at what accuracy threshold does human review become unnecessary? This is what the prototype is designed to test

**Knowledge graph layer — Wikibase**

- Domain-specific Wikibase instance following [TIB/NFDI4Culture model](https://doi.org/10.5334/johd.440): specialized data lives locally, federates to Wikidata for entity metadata
- Output: YAML provenance logs + Wikibase structured data, both CC0

-----

### Starting Scope

**Corpus:** First Amendment free speech cases — bounded, dense citation graph, pedagogically legible for a law school audience.

**Seed cases:** *Schenck v. United States* (1919) → *Brandenburg v. Ohio* (1969) → *Ashcroft v. Free Speech Coalition* (2002). Three cases, ~80 years, tractable citation chain.

**Target output per case:** Wikidata item with holding statement; citation spans extracted from opinion text; for each citation, the proposition for which it is cited with confidence level; YAML provenance log.

-----

### Key Research Questions

1. **Can an LLM reliably extract citation semantics from legal text?** (Proposition, relationship type, supporting passage — this is the central question the prototype is designed to answer)
1. **At what accuracy threshold does human review become unnecessary?** (The prototype builds a human-verified ground truth for the seed cases to measure this)
1. **Does S2ORC-style claim extraction transfer to legal text?** (Opinions have different structure — per curiam sections, footnotes, dissents)
1. **Does Wikidata have adequate case coverage?** (If not, what needs adding before federation and Wikipedia gap-finding work?)
