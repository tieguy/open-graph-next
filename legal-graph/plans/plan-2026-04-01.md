## Constitutional Caselaw Knowledge Graph — Prototype Plan

### Conceptual Anchor: Semantic Scholar for Law

The Allen Institute's [Semantic Scholar](https://www.semanticscholar.org) demonstrates that a large document corpus can be transformed into a structured knowledge layer by extracting citation context, classifying citation intent, and making the result queryable in ways the raw corpus doesn't support. Their [S2ORC pipeline](https://github.com/allenai/s2orc) structures full-text papers; their citation intent classifier labels edges as background/method/result; [OpenScholar](https://github.com/AkariAsai/OpenScholar) uses the result to help researchers find gaps in literature coverage.

This prototype extends that model to constitutional caselaw, with three differences:

- **Domain**: Law rather than science — citation treatment categories follow the established citator taxonomy (see below)
- **Corpus**: Fully public domain (US federal caselaw via [Free Law Project](https://free.law)), not limited to open-access papers
- **Verification**: SIFT-based process producing a verified annotation layer — with human approval as a baseline to test against, not assumed to be necessary at scale
- **Output license**: CC0, enabling reuse as AI training data

-----

### The Three Outputs

**1. A verified benchmark dataset for legal citation accuracy** — training and evaluation data for the task "does this AI-generated legal claim accurately characterize its cited source," built on public domain opinions, released under CC0. Addresses the worst failure mode of commercial legal AI (Harvey, Lexis+AI, Westlaw AI), which current benchmarks don't adequately test.

**2. A demonstration that the method generalizes to normative/interpretive knowledge** — caselaw is harder than drug interactions (ground truth is contested) but more tractable than fully open-ended normative questions. Proving the method here extends its credibility to the harder domains the broader project needs.

**3. A structured knowledge base enabling LLM-assisted gap-finding for Wikipedia legal articles** — not content generation, but a queryable layer that helps editors identify where Wikipedia's legal coverage is thin, unsourced, or inaccurate, and surfaces primary sources to fill those gaps. The legal analog to what OpenScholar does for scientific literature.

-----

### What Free Law Project Provides vs. What This Adds

[Free Law Project](https://free.law) has built the citation graph — opinion A cites opinion B, with character offsets. That's the edge. What nobody has in open infrastructure is the *semantic content* of the edge: what proposition does A take from B, and is that characterization accurate? This project adds the verified annotation layer on top of FLP's corpus — the same relationship as OpenCitations to Crossref. FLP is the upstream data provider; this is the semantic layer they've deliberately left out of scope.

-----

### Citation Treatment Taxonomy

We adopt the established legal citator treatment categories used by Shepard's Citations and KeyCite. These categories are standard legal vocabulary — what's proprietary in those systems is the labeled data, not the taxonomy itself. Building an open CC0-licensed labeled corpus using these established categories is the core contribution.

#### Treatment Categories (substantive engagement with cited case)

| Category | Definition | Example from seed corpus |
|----------|-----------|--------------------------|
| **Followed** | Citing court adopts the reasoning or holding of the cited case | *Ashcroft* following *Brandenburg*'s "imminent lawless action" test |
| **Distinguished** | Citing court explains why the cited case doesn't control the present facts | A court distinguishing *Schenck* on the basis that the speech wasn't linked to wartime obstruction |
| **Overruled** | Citing court explicitly rejects the cited case | *Brandenburg* overruling *Schenck*'s "clear and present danger" test |
| **Criticized** | Citing court disagrees without formally overruling (often in dicta or dissent) | Dissent criticizing continued reliance on a doctrinal test |
| **Questioned** | Citing court expresses doubt about continued validity | "The reasoning of *X* is difficult to reconcile with..." |
| **Limited** | Citing court narrows the scope of the cited case | "We read *X* to apply only in the context of..." |
| **Explained** | Citing court clarifies or interprets the cited case without taking a substantive position | "In *Schenck*, the Court first addressed wartime speech restrictions..." |
| **Harmonized** | Citing court reconciles apparent inconsistency between the cited case and another authority | "Read together, *X* and *Y* stand for the proposition that..." |

#### Appellate History (same-case procedural chain — tracked separately)

Affirmed · Reversed · Modified · Vacated · Superseded · Remanded

These describe the procedural relationship within a single case's appellate journey, not substantive engagement between different cases. Tracked as metadata on the case entity, not as citation treatment edges.

#### Context Dimensions (tracked per citation instance)

- **Opinion section**: Majority / Concurrence / Dissent / Per curiam
- **Depth of discussion**: Analyzed (extended discussion) / Discussed (paragraph+) / Mentioned (passing reference)
- **Proposition extracted**: The specific legal claim for which the cited case is invoked
- **Supporting passage**: Location in the cited opinion that supports the proposition

### The Stack

**Document layer — Free Law Project**

- Full text of US federal caselaw, public domain; accessible via CourtListener API (requires API key) or bulk data
- [Eyecite](https://github.com/freelawproject/eyecite) (FLP's own citation parser) used to enumerate all citations within the seed case corpus — not to do semantic analysis, just to ensure complete coverage of the citation graph within the test suite
- Parallel: [Harvard Caselaw Access Project](https://case.law) for historical depth

**Entity layer — Wikidata**

- Cases, courts, justices already partially represented; Q-IDs as persistent identifiers
- Gaps to fill: holding statements, doctrinal relationships, citation treatment edges (P2860)

**Verification layer — LLM extraction + human baseline**

- Core research question: given opinion text containing a citation, can an LLM reliably identify the proposition for which the cited case is used, classify the treatment type using the standard citator taxonomy, and locate the supporting passage in the cited opinion?
- Human verification used to build a ground-truth baseline for the seed cases — not assumed necessary at scale, but required to measure LLM accuracy
- Interesting failure modes are legal-specific: hallucinating that a case stands for a proposition it doesn't hold; mischaracterizing a distinguishing as a following; correctly identifying the treatment type but locating the wrong passage in the cited opinion
- Key open question: at what accuracy threshold does human review become unnecessary? This is what the prototype is designed to test

**Knowledge graph layer — Wikibase**

- Domain-specific Wikibase instance following [TIB/NFDI4Culture model](https://doi.org/10.5334/johd.440): specialized data lives locally, federates to Wikidata for entity metadata
- Output: YAML provenance logs + Wikibase structured data, both CC0

-----

### Starting Scope

**Corpus:** First Amendment free speech cases — bounded, dense citation graph, pedagogically legible for a law school audience.

**Seed cases:** *Schenck v. United States* (1919) → *Brandenburg v. Ohio* (1969) → *Ashcroft v. Free Speech Coalition* (2002). Three cases, ~80 years, tractable citation chain.

**Target output per case:** Wikidata item with holding statement; citation spans extracted from opinion text; for each citation, the proposition for which it is cited, treatment type from the standard taxonomy, depth of discussion, opinion section, and confidence level; YAML provenance log.

-----

### Key Research Questions

1. **Can an LLM reliably extract citation treatment from legal text?** (Proposition, treatment type, supporting passage — this is the central question the prototype is designed to answer)
1. **At what accuracy threshold does human review become unnecessary?** (The prototype builds a human-verified ground truth for the seed cases to measure this)
1. **Does S2ORC-style claim extraction transfer to legal text?** (Opinions have different structure — per curiam sections, footnotes, dissents)
1. **Does Wikidata have adequate case coverage?** (If not, what needs adding before federation and Wikipedia gap-finding work?)

- [ ] Human-verify LLM extraction results for seed cases

#### Phase 4: Scale Testing

- [ ] Expand corpus beyond seed cases within First Amendment domain
- [ ] Measure LLM accuracy at scale against human baseline

- [ ] Assess Wikipedia gap-finding feasibility
