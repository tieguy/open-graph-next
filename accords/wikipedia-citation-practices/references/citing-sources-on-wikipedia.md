Wikipedia has developed a sophisticated citation system through years of community practice. This document covers the mechanics: how citations are formatted, where they are placed, when they are required, and what tools support them.

## Inline citations (WP:INCITE)

The preferred method of citation on Wikipedia is the *inline citation*: a reference placed immediately after the claim it supports, using a `<ref>` tag that generates a numbered footnote.

```wikitext
Douglas Adams was born in Cambridge.<ref>{{cite book |last=Simpson |first=M. J.
|title=Hitchhiker: A Biography of Douglas Adams |year=2003 |page=15}}</ref>
```

This renders as a superscript number in the article text, with the full citation appearing in a "References" section at the bottom of the page (generated by a `{{reflist}}` template or `<references />` tag).

### Key principles

- **Proximity.** The citation should appear as close as possible to the claim it supports — typically at the end of the sentence or clause containing the claim.
- **One claim, one citation.** Each distinct claim should have its own citation. A single citation at the end of a paragraph does not adequately source all claims in that paragraph.
- **Named references.** When the same source supports multiple claims, use named references to avoid duplicating the full citation:
  ```wikitext
  First claim.<ref name="Simpson2003">{{cite book |...}}</ref>
  ...
  Later claim.<ref name="Simpson2003" />
  ```

See: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#Inline_citations

## General references vs. inline citations

Wikipedia recognizes two citation styles:

- **Inline citations** (described above): tied to specific claims via `<ref>` tags. Strongly preferred.
- **General references**: sources listed in a bibliography-style section without being tied to specific claims. Acceptable for well-established, uncontroversial topics but insufficient for contentious material, BLP content, or featured article candidates.

Articles that rely solely on general references are often tagged with `{{more footnotes}}` or `{{no footnotes}}` to request conversion to inline citations.

## Citation templates

Wikipedia provides a family of *citation templates* that standardize reference formatting and produce machine-readable metadata. The major templates are:

| Template | Use for |
|---|---|
| `{{cite web}}` | Web pages, online articles |
| `{{cite news}}` | Newspaper and magazine articles |
| `{{cite journal}}` | Academic journal articles |
| `{{cite book}}` | Books and book chapters |
| `{{cite conference}}` | Conference papers and proceedings |
| `{{cite report}}` | Government and organizational reports |
| `{{cite press release}}` | Press releases (note: generally not independent sources) |
| `{{cite encyclopedia}}` | Encyclopedia entries |
| `{{cite AV media}}` | Audio and video recordings |

### Common parameters

Most citation templates share a core set of parameters:

- `|title=` — title of the source (required)
- `|url=` — URL of the source (if available online)
- `|date=` or `|year=` — publication date
- `|access-date=` — date the URL was last accessed (required for web sources)
- `|last=` / `|first=` — author name (supports `|last1=`, `|first1=`, `|last2=`, etc.)
- `|publisher=` — name of the publisher
- `|pages=` or `|page=` — page number(s) for the specific claim
- `|doi=`, `|isbn=`, `|pmid=`, `|issn=` — persistent identifiers
- `|archive-url=` / `|archive-date=` — Wayback Machine or other archive links
- `|quote=` — a direct quotation from the source supporting the claim

### Citation style consistency

Wikipedia does not mandate a single citation style. However, articles should be internally consistent. The three major styles in use are:

1. **Citation templates** (most common): `{{cite web}}`, `{{cite book}}`, etc.
2. **Citation Style 1 (CS1)**: the default rendering of citation templates
3. **Citation Style 2 (CS2)**: an alternative rendering activated by `{{citation}}` instead of `{{cite X}}`

An article should not mix styles. If an article already uses a particular style, new citations should match it.

See: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_templates

## When to cite (WP:WHENTOREF)

Not every sentence needs a citation. Wikipedia's guidelines identify specific situations that require or strongly recommend citations:

### Always cite

- **Direct quotations.** Any quoted material must cite the source and page number.
- **Material challenged or likely to be challenged.** If any editor has challenged a claim or if the claim is contentious, it must be cited.
- **Contentious material about living persons.** All contentious claims in BLPs must be cited (see WP:BLP).
- **Statistics and quantitative claims.** Numbers, dates, measurements, and data points should be cited.
- **Claims about specific events.** "On March 3, the company announced..." requires a source.

### Strongly recommended

- **Material that could reasonably be questioned.** Even if not yet challenged, claims that a reasonable reader might doubt should be preemptively cited.
- **Statements of opinion attributed to a source.** "According to Smith, the reform was effective" needs a citation to Smith's publication.
- **Claims about causation.** "X caused Y" or "X led to Y" almost always require a source.

### Generally not required

- **Uncontroversial common knowledge.** "Paris is the capital of France" does not need a citation, though one would not be harmful.
- **Statements already supported by nearby cited material.** If a paragraph's claims flow from a single source, a citation at the end of the paragraph may suffice for uncontentious material (though inline citations are still preferred).

See: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:When_to_cite

## Citation needed (WP:CN)

The `{{citation needed}}` tag — rendered as the familiar "[citation needed]" in article text — is Wikipedia's mechanism for marking unsourced claims. It is one of the most-used maintenance templates, appearing on over 600,000 pages.

### Usage

```wikitext
The building was completed in 1923.{{citation needed|date=March 2026}}
```

The `|date=` parameter enables tracking how long the tag has been in place. Material tagged for an extended period without a source being added may eventually be removed.

### Responding to citation needed tags

When adding a citation that addresses a `{{citation needed}}` tag:

1. Add the inline citation supporting the claim.
2. Remove the `{{citation needed}}` tag manually. No automated process does this.
3. If the claim cannot be supported by a reliable source, consider removing the claim rather than leaving the tag indefinitely.

### Related tags

- `{{better source needed}}` — the claim has a source, but it is weak or inappropriate.
- `{{failed verification}}` — an editor checked the cited source and it does not support the claim.
- `{{unreliable source?}}` — flags a source whose reliability is in question.
- `{{primary source inline}}` — flags reliance on a primary source where a secondary source would be more appropriate.

## Persistent identifiers and access

Good citations maximize the chance that future readers can access the source:

- **DOIs** (Digital Object Identifiers): stable identifiers for academic publications. Use `|doi=` in citation templates.
- **ISBNs**: stable identifiers for books. Use `|isbn=`.
- **PMIDs/PMCIDs**: PubMed identifiers for biomedical literature. Use `|pmid=` or `|pmc=`.
- **Archive URLs**: Wayback Machine or other web archive links. Use `|archive-url=` with `|archive-date=`.
- **Access indicators**: `|url-access=subscription` or `|url-access=registration` signals that the source requires payment or an account.

InternetArchiveBot automatically archives many URLs and replaces dead links with archived versions, but proactively adding archive links when creating citations is good practice.
