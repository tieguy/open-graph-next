---
title: Citing to verifiable, reliable sources
---

English Wikipedia's content policies determine what material belongs in the encyclopedia. For citation work, three policies are foundational: *verifiability* (including *reliability*), *no original research*, and *neutral point of view*. This document focuses on the first two and on the sourcing standards that support them.

## Verifiability (WP:V)

The threshold for inclusion in Wikipedia is *verifiability*, not truth. This distinction is central: a claim does not belong in Wikipedia because it is true, but because a reader can check it against a published, reliable source.

Wikipedia's content is determined by published information rather than editors' beliefs, experiences, or previously unpublished ideas. Even if an editor is sure something is true, it must have been published in a reliable source before it can be added. If reliable sources disagree, the article should maintain a neutral point of view and present what the various sources say, giving each side its due weight.

### What verifiability requires

The policy has several practical consequences:

- **Material must be attributable.** A fact or claim is "verifiable" if a reliable source that supports it *could* be cited, even if no citation currently appears.
- **Some material must always be attributed.** Four types of information must be accompanied by inline citations to reliable sources that directly support the material: (1) direct quotations, (2) material whose verifiability has been challenged, (3) material whose verifiability is likely to be challenged, and (4) contentious material about living and recently deceased persons.
- **The burden of evidence lies with the editor.** The person adding or restoring material is responsible for providing an inline citation to a reliable source that directly supports the contribution. Other editors are not obligated to search for sources on their behalf.
- **Unsourced material may be removed.** Any editor may remove unsourced or poorly sourced material at any time, especially in biographies of living persons (WP:BLP), where contentious material must be removed immediately — not tagged, not moved to the talk page, but removed.
- **"Verifiable" does not mean "verified."** Wikipedia does not require that every editor personally verify every source. But it must be *possible* for any reader to do so.

### Verifiability, not truth (WP:VNT)

The phrase "the threshold for inclusion is verifiability, not truth" was the explicit statement of WP:V policy until 2012, when it was reworded for clarity. The core principle remains: verifiability is a *necessary* condition for inclusion but not a *sufficient* one. Sources must also be appropriate, used carefully, and balanced relative to other sources per Wikipedia's policy on due weight.

This principle has several important dimensions:

- **Editors are not truth-finders.** Wikipedia editors do not make judgments about what is true and what is false. They determine what can be verified in a reliable source and whether it otherwise belongs in Wikipedia. An editor who finds a claim supported only by dated sources should look for more modern sources with updated information, rather than simply removing the material based on personal knowledge.
- **Truth is not always clear-cut.** In many fields — particularly the social sciences — there is no single "truth," only facts, opinions, facts about opinions, and opinions about opinions. Wikipedia must not present a fact as an opinion, nor an opinion as a fact.
- **Reliable sources can be wrong.** Publication in a reliable source is not sufficient to establish that a view is correct. Reliable sources may be outdated, disputed by other sources, or contain simple errors. When a source is clearly mistaken on a minor detail (e.g., misspelling a name), the error should be silently corrected, not used to manufacture a dispute. But when a source makes a substantive claim that conflicts with other reliable sources, the conflict should be described without the editor taking a side.
- **Majority and minority views.** If there is an almost universally accepted viewpoint and a tiny minority one, the minority opinion may be omitted, and the majority viewpoint described as fact. However, representing a majority viewpoint as fact does not mean Wikipedia *endorses* it as true — it means the preponderance of reliable sources supports it.

The verifiability policy interacts with Wikipedia's other content policies. A claim may be verifiable but still excluded if it violates *neutral point of view* (WP:NPOV) or constitutes original research.

## Reliable sources (WP:RS)

Not all published sources are considered reliable. Wikipedia evaluates sources based on their editorial processes, not on their conclusions. The key factors are:

- **Editorial oversight.** Sources with robust editorial processes — fact-checking, editorial review, corrections policies — are preferred. Academic peer-reviewed journals, major newspapers, and established reference works generally qualify.
- **Reputation for accuracy.** A publication's track record matters. Sources with a history of fabrication, errors, or extreme bias are not reliable regardless of their editorial structure.
- **Independence from the subject.** Sources should be *independent* or *third-party* (WP:THIRDPARTY) — meaning they have no financial, personal, or organizational relationship to the subject of the claim. A company's own press release about its founding date may be a useful primary source but is not independent.
- **Context matters.** A source may be reliable for some claims but not others. A tabloid newspaper might reliably report a public figure's attendance at an event but would not be reliable for medical claims.

### Source type hierarchy

Wikipedia distinguishes three types of sources:

- **Primary sources** are original materials: court records, census data, published interviews, datasets, official organizational output. They are acceptable for straightforward, uncontroversial claims ("The company was founded in 1998" supported by the company's SEC filing). They should not be used to make interpretive or analytical claims.
- **Secondary sources** analyze, interpret, or synthesize primary sources: news reporting, scholarly articles, biographies, book reviews. These are generally the strongest sources for Wikipedia because they involve editorial judgment and context.
- **Tertiary sources** compile and summarize secondary sources: encyclopedias, textbooks, databases. They are useful for establishing the broad consensus on a topic but may be too general for specific claims.

The preference ordering is: secondary sources over primary sources over tertiary sources, with significant context-dependence.

### Questionable and self-published sources

Certain categories of sources are generally not reliable:

- **Questionable sources** have a poor reputation for checking facts, lack meaningful editorial oversight, or have an apparent conflict of interest. These include websites and publications expressing views widely considered promotional or extremist, or that rely heavily on unsubstantiated gossip, rumor, or personal opinion. Predatory open-access journals are also questionable due to absent quality control in peer review. Questionable sources should be used only as sources for material about *themselves*.
- **Self-published sources** — personal websites, open wikis, blogs, content farms, podcasts, social media posts, forum postings — are largely not acceptable. Anyone can create a personal web page or self-publish a book. Self-published sources *may* be considered reliable when published by an established subject-matter expert whose work in the relevant field has previously been published by reliable, independent publications. But even then, caution applies: if the information is suitable for inclusion, someone else will likely have published it in an independent source. Self-published sources must never be used as third-party sources about living people.
- **Self-published or questionable sources about themselves** may be used in articles about themselves or their activities, provided the material is not unduly self-serving, does not involve claims about third parties or unrelated events, and there is no reasonable doubt about its authenticity. This applies to social media as well.

### Circular sourcing: Wikipedia and its mirrors

Wikipedia must not be used as a source for Wikipedia. User-generated content cannot validate itself. This prohibition extends to websites that mirror Wikipedia content and to publications that rely on material from Wikipedia. If a claim in a Wikipedia article is cited to another Wikipedia article, editors should find the underlying reliable source and cite it directly.

### Exceptional claims require exceptional sources (WP:REDFLAG)

Certain types of claims trigger heightened sourcing requirements:

- Claims that are surprising or apparently important but not widely covered
- Claims that are politically or scientifically contentious
- Claims about living or recently deceased persons (WP:BLP)
- Claims that contradict well-established scholarly consensus
- Claims promoted primarily by the subject of the article (self-serving sources)

For these claims, multiple high-quality, independent sources are expected. A single source, no matter how reliable, may not be sufficient.

### Accessibility of sources

Sources should not be rejected just because they are difficult or costly to access. Some reliable sources are behind paywalls, available only in print, or held in special collections. Non-English sources are also acceptable — English-language sources are preferred when available and of equal quality, but a citation to a non-English reliable source is valid. When quoting non-English material, a translation into English should accompany the quote, with professionally published translations preferred over editor translations, which in turn are preferred over machine translations.

See: [Wikipedia:Reliable sources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources)

## No original research (WP:NOR)

Wikipedia articles must not contain *original research*: material for which no reliable, published source exists. This includes:

- **Novel analysis or synthesis.** Editors must not combine sources to reach a conclusion that none of them individually state. If Source A says "X happened" and Source B says "Y happened," an editor must not write "X caused Y" unless a reliable source explicitly makes that connection.
- **Unpublished facts or data.** Personal observations, experiments, interviews, or surveys conducted by editors cannot be cited.
- **Novel interpretations.** Applying a published theory to a new situation, even if the application seems logical, constitutes original research if no source has published that application.

The boundary between legitimate editorial judgment (selecting and organizing material from sources) and original research (drawing conclusions beyond what sources state) is one of Wikipedia's most frequent editorial disputes.

See: [Wikipedia:No original research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research)

## Biographies of living persons (WP:BLP)

Wikipedia applies its most stringent sourcing standards to articles about living people. The policy extends beyond article space to all Wikimedia content, including talk pages, user pages, and edit summaries.

Key requirements:

- **Contentious material must be sourced.** Unsourced or poorly sourced contentious material about a living person must be removed immediately — not tagged, not moved to the talk page, but removed.
- **High-quality sources required.** Material must be sourced to reliable, published sources. Tabloids, self-published sources, and anonymous sources are generally insufficient for contentious claims.
- **Presumption of privacy.** Not all verifiable information about a living person belongs in Wikipedia. Editors must exercise judgment about whether including sourced material serves the encyclopedic purpose.
- **Subjects may request removal.** Living persons who are the subject of a Wikipedia article may request removal of unsourced negative material, and such requests should be taken seriously.

BLP is enforced aggressively. Editors who repeatedly add unsourced contentious material to biographies of living persons may be blocked.

See: [Wikipedia:Biographies of living persons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Biographies_of_living_persons)

## Practical implications for citation work

When evaluating or adding citations, apply these questions:

1. **Is the source reliable for this specific claim?** A source may be reliable in general but not for the particular claim being supported.
2. **Is the source independent of the subject?** First-party sources have their uses but should be supplemented by independent sources.
3. **Does the source actually say what the article claims?** Verify that the cited source supports the specific claim, not a related but different one.
4. **Is this an exceptional claim?** If so, does the sourcing meet the heightened standard?
5. **Is there synthesis?** Are multiple sources being combined to imply a conclusion none of them individually state?
6. **Is this a BLP issue?** Any contentious claim about a living person requires extra care.
7. **Is the source self-published or questionable?** If so, is it being used only for claims about itself, and does it meet the criteria for such use?
8. **Could truth and verifiability diverge here?** Remember that the goal is to reflect what reliable sources say, not to determine what is true. If reliable sources conflict, present the disagreement rather than adjudicating it.
