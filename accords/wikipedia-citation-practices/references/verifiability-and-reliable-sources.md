---
title: Verifiability and reliable sources
---

Wikipedia's content policies form a hierarchy that determines what material belongs in the encyclopedia. For citation work, three policies are foundational: *verifiability*, *no original research*, and *neutral point of view*. This document focuses on the first two and on the sourcing standards that support them.

## Verifiability (WP:V)

The threshold for inclusion in Wikipedia is *verifiability*, not truth. This distinction is central: a claim does not belong in Wikipedia because it is true, but because a reader can check it against a published, reliable source.

The policy has several practical consequences:

- **Material must be attributable.** Any statement that is or is likely to be challenged must cite a reliable, published source. Material that cannot be attributed to a reliable source should be removed.
- **The burden of evidence lies with the editor.** The person adding or restoring material is responsible for providing an inline citation to a reliable source. Other editors are not obligated to search for sources on their behalf.
- **Unsourced material may be removed.** Any editor may remove unsourced or poorly sourced material at any time, especially in biographies of living persons (WP:BLP), where the standard is strictly enforced.
- **"Verifiable" does not mean "verified."** Wikipedia does not require that every editor personally verify every source. But it must be *possible* for any reader to do so.

The verifiability policy interacts with Wikipedia's other content policies. A claim may be verifiable but still excluded if it violates *neutral point of view* (WP:NPOV) or constitutes original research.

See: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability

## Reliable sources (WP:RS)

Not all published sources are considered reliable. Wikipedia evaluates sources based on their editorial processes, not on their conclusions. The key factors are:

- **Editorial oversight.** Sources with robust editorial processes — fact-checking, editorial review, corrections policies — are preferred. Academic peer-reviewed journals, major newspapers, and established reference works generally qualify.
- **Reputation for accuracy.** A publication's track record matters. Sources with a history of fabrication, errors, or extreme bias are not reliable regardless of their editorial structure.
- **Independence from the subject.** Sources should be *independent* or *third-party* (WP:THIRDPARTY) — meaning they have no financial, personal, or organizational relationship to the subject of the claim. A company's own press release about its founding date may be a useful primary source but is not independent.
- **Context matters.** A source may be reliable for some claims but not others. A tabloid newspaper might reliably report a public figure's attendance at an event but would not be reliable for medical claims.

### Source type hierarchy

Wikipedia distinguishes three types of sources:

- **Primary sources** are original materials: court records, census data, published interviews, datasets, official organizational output. They are acceptable for straightforward, uncontroversial claims ("The company was founded in 1998" supported by the company's SEC filing). They should not be used to make interpretive or analytical claims.
- **Secondary sources** analyze, interpret, or synthesize primary sources: news reporting, scholarly articles, biographies, book reviews. These are generally the strongest sources for Wikipedia because they involve editorial judgment and context.
- **Tertiary sources** compile and summarize secondary sources: encyclopedias, textbooks, databases. They are useful for establishing the broad consensus on a topic but may be too general for specific claims.

The preference ordering is: secondary sources over primary sources over tertiary sources, with significant context-dependence.

### Exceptional claims require exceptional sources (WP:REDFLAG)

Certain types of claims trigger heightened sourcing requirements:

- Claims that are surprising or apparently important but not widely covered
- Claims that are politically or scientifically contentious
- Claims about living or recently deceased persons (WP:BLP)
- Claims that contradict well-established scholarly consensus
- Claims promoted primarily by the subject of the article (self-serving sources)

For these claims, multiple high-quality, independent sources are expected. A single source, no matter how reliable, may not be sufficient.

See: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources

## No original research (WP:NOR)

Wikipedia articles must not contain *original research*: material for which no reliable, published source exists. This includes:

- **Novel analysis or synthesis.** Editors must not combine sources to reach a conclusion that none of them individually state. If Source A says "X happened" and Source B says "Y happened," an editor must not write "X caused Y" unless a reliable source explicitly makes that connection.
- **Unpublished facts or data.** Personal observations, experiments, interviews, or surveys conducted by editors cannot be cited.
- **Novel interpretations.** Applying a published theory to a new situation, even if the application seems logical, constitutes original research if no source has published that application.

The boundary between legitimate editorial judgment (selecting and organizing material from sources) and original research (drawing conclusions beyond what sources state) is one of Wikipedia's most frequent editorial disputes.

See: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research

## Biographies of living persons (WP:BLP)

Wikipedia applies its most stringent sourcing standards to articles about living people. The policy extends beyond article space to all Wikimedia content, including talk pages, user pages, and edit summaries.

Key requirements:

- **Contentious material must be sourced.** Unsourced or poorly sourced contentious material about a living person must be removed immediately — not tagged, not moved to the talk page, but removed.
- **High-quality sources required.** Material must be sourced to reliable, published sources. Tabloids, self-published sources, and anonymous sources are generally insufficient for contentious claims.
- **Presumption of privacy.** Not all verifiable information about a living person belongs in Wikipedia. Editors must exercise judgment about whether including sourced material serves the encyclopedic purpose.
- **Subjects may request removal.** Living persons who are the subject of a Wikipedia article may request removal of unsourced negative material, and such requests should be taken seriously.

BLP is enforced aggressively. Editors who repeatedly add unsourced contentious material to biographies of living persons may be blocked.

See: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Biographies_of_living_persons

## Practical implications for citation work

When evaluating or adding citations, apply these questions:

1. **Is the source reliable for this specific claim?** A source may be reliable in general but not for the particular claim being supported.
2. **Is the source independent of the subject?** First-party sources have their uses but should be supplemented by independent sources.
3. **Does the source actually say what the article claims?** Verify that the cited source supports the specific claim, not a related but different one.
4. **Is this an exceptional claim?** If so, does the sourcing meet the heightened standard?
5. **Is there synthesis?** Are multiple sources being combined to imply a conclusion none of them individually state?
6. **Is this a BLP issue?** Any contentious claim about a living person requires extra care.
